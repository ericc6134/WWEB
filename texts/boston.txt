BOSTON &mdash; The day after two powerful bombs exploded near the finish line of the Boston Marathon, a mile-square area around Copley Square here remained cordoned off as a crime scene, and officials still had no one in custody. Investigators searched a house in a nearby suburb late Monday night, but later said the search had proved fruitless.        </p>

<h6 class="credit">Justin Lane/European Pressphoto Agency</h6>
<p class="caption">Police officers near the finish line of the Boston Marathon as the investigation into the dual bombing continued on Tuesday.                            </p>
</div>
  
</div>
<div id="readerscomment" class="inlineLeft"></div>
<div class="articleBody">
 <p itemprop="articleBody">
Hundreds of runners who had expected to leave Boston on Tuesday morning with a sense of triumph after a night of celebration left instead with heavy hearts after at least three people were killed. The bombings also sent 176 people to area hospitals, including 17 who were critically injured, Police Commissioner Edward Davis of Boston said Tuesday.        </p><p itemprop="articleBody">
Among the dead was an 8-year-old boy, Martin Richard, of Dorchester, according to Conor Yunits, a family spokesman. He had been watching the marathon with his family; his mother and a sister were badly injured. The names of the other victims have not been made public.        </p><p itemprop="articleBody">
Late Monday night, law enforcement officials descended on an apartment building in the suburb of Revere, about five miles north of Copley Square, linked to a man the police took into custody near the scene of the bombings. But on Tuesday morning, one law enforcement official said investigators had determined that the man, who was injured in the blast and was questioned at the hospital, was not involved in the attack.        </p><p itemprop="articleBody">
A few details about the explosives emerged on Tuesday. Representative Michael McCaul, a Texas Republican who heads the House Homeland Security Committee, said the authorities believe the explosives were likely a &ldquo;pressure-cooker device,&rdquo; similar to improvised explosive weapons used against American troops in Iraq and Afghanistan. Mr. McCaul, a former federal prosecutor who received briefings Tuesday morning from the F.B.I. and Homeland Security officials, said the authorities still did not know whether the attack was a foreign or domestic plot.        </p><p itemprop="articleBody">
Surgeons at Boston hospitals said during a televised news conferences on Tuesday that the devices were packed with small pellets and sharp &ldquo;nail-like&rdquo; objects that were designed to maim victims.        </p><p itemprop="articleBody">
Congressman Stephen Lynch, a Democrat of Massachusetts, said doctors had identified material lodged in a survivor&rsquo;s leg as a ball bearing.        </p><p itemprop="articleBody">
The presence of a ball bearing, Mr. Lynch said, along with the timing of the explosions, indicated that the device was probably built by someone with expertise, &ldquo;not some kid in his garage putting something together, I don&rsquo;t think.&rdquo;        </p><p itemprop="articleBody">
Mr. Lynch characterized the choice of the material as deliberate. &ldquo;This is not a device like Oklahoma City,&rdquo; he said. &ldquo;That was to bring the building down. The ball bearings are meant as antipersonnel munitions. They&rsquo;re trying to cause carnage here.&rdquo;        </p><p itemprop="articleBody">
The authorities have not announced any arrests, and so far, no one has claimed responsibility as the police conduct what they said was &ldquo;a criminal investigation that is a potential terrorist investigation.&rdquo;        </p><p itemprop="articleBody">
Law enforcement officials pleaded at a briefing Tuesday morning for anyone who took pictures or video of the finish line at the time of the blast to submit them <a href="mailto:boston@ic.fbi.gov">boston@ic.fbi.gov</a> or to call 1-800-CALL-FBI (1-800-225-5324).        </p><p itemprop="articleBody">
The plea underscored just how pervasive cameras have become at events like the marathon and how crucial they can be in helping the police piece together crucial pieces of evidence. But it may also suggest how few clues the authorities have otherwise.        </p><p itemprop="articleBody">
The police also said they were examining footage from nearby security cameras frame by frame as they continue their search for the identity of the person or persons who placed explosive devices at the end of the 26.2-mile course.        </p><p itemprop="articleBody">
Commissioner Davis said that officials were gradually reducing the size of the crime scene, which on Tuesday stretched for 12 blocks in Copley Square, down from 15 blocks on Monday. He said it was the most complex crime scene in the history of the department.        </p><p itemprop="articleBody">
City streets that normally would be clogged at rush hour were largely deserted on Tuesday except for a cold wind and a few runners out for a morning jog. &ldquo;It&rsquo;s very surreal,&rdquo; said Mary Ollinger, 32, who works at Wentworth Institute of Technology. &ldquo;The streets are empty and the Common is filled with media trucks.&rdquo;        </p><div id="pageLinks"><ul id="pageNumbers"><li> 1 </li><li> <a onClick="s_code_linktrack('Article-MultiPagePageNum2');" title="Page 2" href="/2013/04/17/us/officials-investigate-boston-explosions.html?pagewanted=2&_r=0&hp">2</a> </li></ul><a class="next" onClick="s_code_linktrack('Article-MultiPage-Next');"
